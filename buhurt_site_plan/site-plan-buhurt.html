Home page

The content for the home page includes the history of Burhurt as well as where to go to watch or join a team. What is Buhurt? Buhurt is a type of full-contact combat sport that simulates medieval battles, often involving teams of armored fighters engaging in simulated warfare. Derived from the term "bu hurt," which means "to hit" in Old French, Buhurt is a highly physical and tactical sport where participants wear historical armor and use blunt weapons such as swords, axes, and shields. It is known for its intense and brutal nature, combining elements of martial arts, strategy, and teamwork. Fighters in Buhurt often compete in large-scale battles or one-on-one duels, with the goal of outmaneuvering and overpowering their opponents, all while adhering to rules that ensure safety despite the intensity of the action. Buhurt is both a modern sport and a form of historical reenactment, drawing inspiration from medieval combat techniques and tournaments. Buhurt Competitions 1. Buhurt Training

This page will be the how-to-train to become a Burhurt. There will be specific programs listed as well as different excersize routines. Intro: Getting in shape for Buhurt requires a combination of strength, endurance, agility, and combat-specific conditioning. The training should focus on building muscle strength, cardiovascular endurance, flexibility, and combat skills, all while also preparing you for the intense physicality of full-contact combat. Here's a breakdown of a 3-month, 6-month, and yearlong routine to prepare you for Buhurt. ____ 3-Month Routine (Foundation Phase) The goal of this phase is to build a solid foundation in strength, cardiovascular fitness, and overall endurance, while also starting to introduce combat-specific exercises. Weekly Schedule: • Monday: Full-body strength training (focus on compound movements) • Tuesday: Cardiovascular conditioning + light sparring • Wednesday: Mobility & flexibility + core workout • Thursday: Full-body strength training (focus on explosiveness) • Friday: Endurance training (HIIT, running, or cycling) + combat drills • Saturday: Rest or active recovery (light stretching, yoga, or walking) • Sunday: Skill practice (Buhurt-specific drills, footwork, and defense training) Key Workouts: 1. Strength Training (Full Body) o Squats (3 sets of 8-10 reps) o Deadlifts (3 sets of 6-8 reps) o Push-ups or Bench Press (3 sets of 10-12 reps) o Pull-ups or Lat Pulldowns (3 sets of 8-10 reps) o Overhead Press (3 sets of 8 reps) o Farmer’s Walk (3 rounds of 30-40 meters) 2. Cardiovascular Conditioning o Running: 30 minutes of steady-state running or interval sprints (e.g., 30 seconds fast, 1 minute slow) o Cycling: 45 minutes at a moderate pace 3. Combat Drills o Work on basic swordplay, shield maneuvers, and footwork. o Shadow sparring: Practice movement, blocking, and striking without an opponent. o Practice breathing and maintaining focus during "combat situations." _______ 6-Month Routine (Strength & Endurance Phase) At this stage, you should increase the intensity of your workouts and start focusing on combat-specific strength and conditioning, while maintaining general fitness. Weekly Schedule: • Monday: Strength training (full body with a focus on functional movements) • Tuesday: High-intensity interval training (HIIT) + combat drills • Wednesday: Flexibility and mobility + core strengthening • Thursday: Strength and explosive power (e.g., Olympic lifts) • Friday: Endurance (long runs, rowing, swimming) + sparring or full-body combat practice • Saturday: Active recovery (yoga, swimming, light cycling) • Sunday: Skill practice (Buhurt drills, weapons practice, and tactical combat) Key Workouts: 1. Strength Training (Functional & Compound) o Front Squats (3 sets of 6-8 reps) o Deadlifts (3 sets of 4-6 reps) o Clean and Press (3 sets of 5 reps) o Dumbbell Rows (3 sets of 8-10 reps) o Kettlebell Swings (3 sets of 15 reps) o Turkish Get-ups (3 sets of 5 per side) 2. HIIT Training o Battle Ropes (30 seconds on, 30 seconds off for 10 minutes) o Tire Flips or Sled Push (30 seconds on, 30 seconds off for 10 minutes) o Burpees (3 sets of 15-20) 3. Endurance o Running: 5-10 km steady-state or interval training o Swimming: 45 minutes of interval training in the pool o Rowing: 30-40 minutes with high intensity 4. Combat Drills o Partner Sparring (light contact, focusing on technique and footwork) o Heavy Bag Training (if available, simulate strikes with swords or axes) o Shield Wall Drills (practicing blocking and movement in groups) _____ 12-Month Routine (Peak Performance Phase) Now that you have a solid base of strength, endurance, and combat skills, you should focus on peak performance, improving specific areas, and enhancing your ability to perform under stress. Weekly Schedule: • Monday: Strength training (focus on power and hypertrophy) • Tuesday: Combat-specific training (sparring, weapons drills, footwork) • Wednesday: Cardiovascular endurance (long sessions) + flexibility • Thursday: Strength + explosive training (Olympic lifts, kettlebell) • Friday: Sparring (full-contact drills, Buhurt scenarios) + agility training • Saturday: Rest or active recovery (yoga, swimming) • Sunday: Combat drills (focus on decision-making and fatigue resistance) Key Workouts: 1. Strength Training (Power & Hypertrophy) o Squats (4 sets of 6-8 reps) o Deadlifts (4 sets of 4-6 reps) o Overhead Press (4 sets of 6-8 reps) o Pull-ups (4 sets of 10-12 reps) o Farmers Walks (4 rounds of 40 meters) o Kettlebell Swings (4 sets of 20) 2. Combat-Specific Training o Sparring: Incorporate full contact with a partner, focusing on realistic fight scenarios. o Weapon Drills: Practice different weapons (swords, axes, shields), focusing on fluidity, timing, and reaction. o Tactical Training: Practice movement patterns that simulate battle scenarios—stamina is key. 3. Agility and Reaction Time o Cone Drills (5-10 minutes of short sprints, direction changes) o Box Jumps (3 sets of 10) o Lateral Skater Jumps (3 sets of 15) 4. Endurance (Mental Toughness) o Long Runs: 10-15 km at a moderate pace, or 60 minutes of swimming or cycling. o Obstacle Course: If possible, incorporate military-style obstacle courses for added toughness and agility _____ Diet & Recovery: Throughout all phases, maintaining a balanced diet and allowing for adequate recovery is crucial. Focus on: • Protein: Aim for 1.2-2.0 grams per kilogram of body weight to support muscle recovery. • Carbs: Ensure adequate carb intake for energy, especially before and after workouts. • Healthy Fats: Incorporate sources of omega-3 and omega-6 fatty acids for joint and heart health. • Hydration: Stay hydrated, especially when engaging in intense training. Allow at least 7-8 hours of sleep each night, and consider incorporating foam rolling and stretching to improve recovery. Regular massages or physiotherapy may also help prevent injury, especially during heavy training periods.

Buhurt Equipment

This page will basically share the standards of Burhurt fighting that must be followed as well as give a list of different sites to purchase Burhurt worthy armor and weapons. Armor - Body Armor: This includes a breastplate and backplate made of metal, such as steel, to protect the torso. It may also involve additional elements like a cuirass, or a chainmail hauberk, which covers the body and arms for full protection. - Arm & Leg Protection: • Pauldrons: Large shoulder guards that protect the upper arms. • Vambraces: Arm armor that covers the forearms. • Greaves: Metal shin guards that protect the lower legs. • Sabatons: Armored footwear or boots designed to protect the feet. - Gloves: Protective gloves, often made of leather with metal reinforcement, to safeguard the hands and fingers. - Shield: A large, sturdy shield (usually a kite or heater shape) for defense, often made of wood and metal or fully metal, depending on the regulations of the event. Underlayers - Gambeson: Beneath the metal armor, fighters are required to wear padded garments to absorb impacts and reduce the risk of injury. This includes a padded gambeson or jacket, which provides additional comfort and protection. - Neck Protection: A coif or collar, made of padded fabric or metal, is used to protect the neck from strikes. Weapons: - Sword: A longsword or one-handed sword with a blunt edge, typically made of steel but designed to avoid injury during combat. - Axe: A battle axe, designed with a blunt edge, is also a common weapon in Buhurt. - Mace: A weapon with a spiked or flanged head, also used in Buhurt competitions. - Polearms: Weapons such as a halberd or spear may be used, depending on the rules of the specific competition.
